TEACHING FOR DIFFERENTIATION (PT.1): WHY INSIGHT MATTERS
--------------------------------
- investigators vs challengers
	- offer unique and valuable perspectives
	- help navigate alternatives
	- provide ongoing advice or consultation
	- help avoid potential land mines
	- educate on new issues and outcomes
	- easy to buy from
	- widespread support inside customer organization

- help them:
	- cut costs
	- increase revenue
	- penetrate new markets
	- mitigate risk

- great questions are not enough, have great insights too

- not just teaching, but commercial teaching
	- don't just give them free consulting

	1. lead to your unique strengths
	2. challenge customers' assumptions
	3. catalyze action
	4. scale across customers

1. lead to your unique strengths
	- where do you outperform your competition?
	- what's your unique (non-replicable) solution?
	- if you can't differentiate from competition, buyers will bid on price
	- stuff like being innovative, customer-focused, solutions-oriented, green, etc... don't count

2. challenge customers' assumptions
	- reframe customers' thinking
	- know their business better than themselves
	- don't seek "yes, i totally agree!", seek "huh, i never thought of it that way before"

3. catalyze action
	- get them to act
	- convence them they'll get incremental value for yout premium price

4. scale across customers
	- script 2-3 easy-to-remember diagnostic questions to map the right insight to the right customer 
	- small number of powerful insigths > small number of unique solutions > scalable process
	- think about customer segmentation

TEACHING FOR DIFFERENTIATION (PT.2): HOW TO BUILD INSIGHT-LED CONVERSATION
--------------------------------
- a purposeful choreography

1. the warmer
	- assess customer's key challenges (based on research or competitors)
		- "we worked with a number of X similar to yours..."
		- "... and we've found these 3 challenges come up again and again..."
		- "... do you have them, or would you add something else?"

2. the reframe
	- connect their challenges to a bigger problem
	- deliver your "headline" (script this in advance)
	- surprise your customer and make him curious for more information
	
3. rational drowning
	- prove the reframe with data, graphs, tables, and charts
	- quantify the problem
	- the cusromer should say:
		- "wow, i had no idea we were wasting that kind of money!"

4. emotional impact
	- when the customer say your solution make sense but is not for 'them' (because they are 'different'):
		- "i understand you are a little bit different, but let me give you a sense of how we have seen this play out at similar companies..."
	- create an emotional connection
	- make them believe you understand them by playing the "oracle" (based on other customers past experiences)

5. a new way
	- convince them of the solution
	- talk about the solution, not the product
	- how much better their life would be if they behave differently? not buy differently.

6. your solution
	- now it's the time where the supplier (you) enter the conversation.
	- make your solution unique
	- make your solution the natural outgrowth of your previous teaching

- a look in the mirror (don't lead your conversations withe this)
	- what you believe in
	- a review of all your capabilities
	- a list of top partners and customers
	- a mapr of all your locations all over the world
	
- developing a purposeful choreography
	- start from step 6, and reverse engineer
	- add element of surprise to headline
	- then go to step 2, reframe
	- start from benefits #6 and ask yourself "why don't my customers value those benefits already?" #2
	- then go to step 3, make them realize they save money

- building the insight generation machine
	- provide customers with game-changing insight
	- specify and personalize the potential impact of that insight
	- introduce your capabilities as the best possible means to acting on that insight

	1. identify your unique benefits
	2. develop commercial insight that challenges customers' thinking
	3. package commercial insight in compelling messages that "lead to"
	4. equip reps to challenge customers

	- commercial teaching is the key to B2B
	- your teaching pitch must be bold
		- more expansive than ordinary ideas
		- innovative
		- push untested and uniwue approaces
		- risky
		- difficult
	
- commercial teaching case study #1:
	- grainer: ask two questions:
		1. how can I teach my customers to save millions
		2. why should my customers buy from me over anyone else?
		- define your USP and how it can massively help customers

	1. the warmer: 
		- "we know you face many challenges every day, such as..."
		- "ask them so pick one or two of those challenges (the more pressing ones)"
		- "make it less of a presentation and more like two colleagues commiserating about a common challenge"
	2. reframe:
		- "studies show that over 40% of dollars are spent on ___"
		- show graphic when you have one
	3. rational drowning
		- amplify problem with valuable insight (numbers, data,)
	4. emotional impact
		- the "pain chain" slide
		- picture a scenario of what will happen as a consequence of the problem
		- to do that, interview past clients and go through their stories
	5. paint a picture of the new way
		- show them how much they will save/earn by using a new approach
	6. your solution
		- show how you are their best (unique) solution

- taking control of the sale
	- be comfortable talking about money 
	- be comfortable to push customers
	- always think ahead the next step
	- don't let the sale stall
	- push for the next step
	- create momentum, urgency, press
	- create tension, don't release it

- 3 misconceptions about taking control
	- negotiation
		- taking control is not just about negotiation skills
		- take control right at the beginning
		- you don't work if they don't commit
		- don't let them go to your cheeper competitors
		- lead and simplify
	- money
		- push the customer not only on financial term, but also on changing their world vision
		- use constructive tension
		- at any objection, agree and amply
		- you must convince the prospect that the problem is urgent 
	- aggressiveness
		- being aggressive is not taking control
		- don't be passive, be assertive
		- don't offer discount if not asked
		- you set the rules, you are not a beggar
		- the customer is not your enemy, is your ally
		- assert the customer reaction and respond accordingly
		- be respectful
		- don't think customers have more power than reps
		- remember that you are teaching your customer something new
		- you have the experience of hundreds of implementations, you know more than your customer
		- you know the potential of your solution, be proud and don't waste it on unworthy customers
		- always ask for something in return for every request of the customer

- equipping reps to take control
	- don't rush for the closure, expecially in uncomfortable situations
	- challengers thrive in ambiguity
	- be comfortable with silence
	- challengers like tension
	- they embrace objection, not quicly dispose them
	- avoid premature closing
	- develop smart negotiation skills

- taking control case study
	- purposeful planning
		- it's a chess game, don't just plan the next move, think ahead
	- anatomy of a successful negotiaiton
		1. acknowledge and defer
			- "I understand that price is something we need to address, but berfore we do, I'd like to take a moment to make sure I completely understand your needs - so we can make sure we're doing everything we can to make this deal as valuable as possible for you. Is that all right?"
		2. deepen and broaden
			- what else beside price matters?
			- warranty? service plan? expedited shipping? installation?
			- dig deeper for customer's needs
		3. explore and compare
			- after you broadent that universe, start shrinking it down to price asking "what are you looking to achieve with a 20 percent price reduction?"
			- expand your options for negotiation
		4. concede according to plan
			- negotiate low-value stuff first
			- start with meaningful concession first, and then smaller ones
			- you win when both parties win
			- never use "take it or leave it" offers

- taking control happens right at the beginning of the conversation, not at the end
- the solution to overcoming passivity
	- clarity of direction over quick closure
	- create real value within the sales process
