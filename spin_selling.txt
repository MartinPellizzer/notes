SPIN

1. Situation Questions:
	- "How long have you had your present equipment?"
	- "Could you tell me about your company's growth plans?"

2. Problem Questions:
	- "Is this operation difficult to perform?"
	- "Are you worried about the quality you get from your old machine?"

3. Implication Questions:
	- "How will this problem affect your future profitability?"
	- "What effect does this reject rate have on customer satisfaction?"

4. Need Questions:
	- "Would it be useful to speed this operation by 10 percent?"
	- "If we could import the quality of this operation, how would that help you?"

CLOSING
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

1. Investigate more, don't rush, build needs

2. Answer all questions before CTA, ask them for concerns:

	- ME: Well, I think that covers everything, Mr. X. But before we go further, could I check whether there are any areas that you feel I should tell you more about?
	- BUYER: Yes, you haven't mentioned...
	- ME: Then let mecover that now. They way it works...

3. Summatize benefits

4. Proposing commitment - don't ask, propose
	- ME: Then I might suggest that the most logical next step would be for you and your accountant to come and see one of these systems in operation.

INVESTIGATING
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Ask questions to uncover Implied Needs (problems) and develop them into Explicit Needs (actions).

SITUATION QUESTIONS: The fewer the better. Only the neccesary ones (3-5).
	- "What's your position?"
	- "How long have you been here?"
	- "Do you make the purchasing decisions?"
	- "What do you see as your objectives in this area?"

	- "What sort of business do you run?"
	- "Is it growing or shrinking?"
	- "What's your annual sales volume?"
	- "How many people do you employ?"

	- "What equipment are you using at present?"
	- "How long have you had it?"
	- "Is it purchased or leased?"
	- "How many people use it?"

Avoid:
	- "Tell me about your business."
	- "What steps do you go through in making a purchasing decision here?"


PROBLEM QUESTIONS: Better than situation questions, but still not good enough (Implied Needs).
	- "Are you satisfied with your present equipment?"
	- "What are the disadvantages of the way you're handling this now?"
	- "Isn't it difficult to process peak loads with your present system?"
	- "Does this old machine give you reliability problems?"


IMPLICATION QUESTIONS: 
	- Develop from PQ
	- Make implied needs larger and more urgent
	- Talk the language of decision makers.

	- "What effect does that have on output?"
	- "Could that lead to increased costs?"
	- "Will it slow down your proposed expansion?"

NEED QUESTIONS: (Explicit Needs LV.2)
	- Develop from IQ.
	- Focus on solutions and not on problems.
	- The customer tells YOU the benefits.
	- NQ reduce objections.
	- Great for internal sales (in HT, an average of 5.4 buyers are involved per sale).


	- "Is it important to you to solve this problem?"
	- "Why would you find this solutions so useful?"
	- "Is there any other way this could help you?"

	- "How do you think a faster machine would help you?"

	- "How would that help?"
	- "Why is it important to solve this problem?"
	- "What benefits do you see?"

SPIN:
	- Check Book for graph

	1. Ask SQ to establish background facts, but don't ask too many as they can bore.
	2. Then quickly move to PQ to explore problems, difficulties, and dissatisfactions (IN).
	3. Then ask IQ to make PQ (IN) grow bigger and more urgent.
	4. Then ask NQ when the buyer agrees the problem is serious enough to justify action (EN). Make the buyer focus on solutions and make him tell YOU the benefits of your solution.


-----------------------
HOW TO USE SPIN:
-----------------------
- Play the part of the problem solver.

- Problem Questions:
	1. Write down at least 3 potential problems you can solve.
	2. Write down some examples of PQ you could ask.

- Implication Questions:
	1. Write down a potential problem the customer is likely to have.
	2. Write down what related difficulties this problem might lead to.
	3. Write down how these difficulties lead to IQ (cost, time, people, legal, etc...);

- Need Questions:
	- Don't ask them too early, build needs with IQ first.
	- Don't ask them where you don't have answers.

	1. Get a friend to help you.
	2. Chose a topic about a need that you believe the other person has.
	3. Ask NQ to get the other person talking about the benefits of the topic under discussion.
		- Why do you think it would be good to have a ...? (fill based on IN from IQ)
		- What would it let us do that we can't do right now?
		- Would anyone else in the company be pleased if we bought one?
		- Do you think it would have any cost advantages compared to ...?

	1. Check for enthusiasm signs in your client.
	2. Ulike IQ which tend to be specific, NQ tend to be general.
		- Why is that important?
		- How would that help?
		- Would it be useful if ...?
		- Is there any other way this could help you? 


-----------------------
GIVING BENEFITS IN MAJOR SALES
-----------------------
- Give benefits only on EN.


-----------------------
PREVENTING OBJECTIONS
-----------------------
- Advantages (aka. "benefits" given on IN) = Objections.


-----------------------
PRELIMINARIES: OPENING THE CALL
-----------------------
- Get permission to ask them questions.
- To do that, you must establish:
	- Who you are.
	- Why you're there.
	- Your right to ask questions.

1. Get down to business quickly.
2. Don't talk about solutions too soon.
3. Concentrate on questions.


-----------------------
TURNING THEORY INTO PRACTICE
-----------------------
1. Practice only one behabior at a time.
2. Try the new behavior at least 3 times.
3. Quantity berore quality.
4. Practice in safe situations.


-----------------------
SUMMARY
-----------------------
- 4 stages of a sales call:
	- Preliminaries.
	- Investigating. ***
	- Demostrating Capability.
	- Obtaining Commitment.

- Preliminaries:
	- No best way to open the call.
	- Get consent to ask questions.

- Investigating:
	- Situation Questions SQ:
		- Facts, background, and situation.
		- Only use them when necessary.
	- Problem Questions PQ:
		- Problems, difficulties, and dissatisfactions.
		- Hunt for problems to find opportunities.
	- Implication Questions IQ:
		- Consequences or effects of a problem (PQ).
		- Ask a lot of them 
		- Grow the problem 
		- Increase the perceived value of your solution.
		- Strongly linked to success in major sales.
	- Need-payoff Questions NQ:
		- Valuse, usefulness, and utility of your solution.
		- Ask a lot of them
		- Strongly linked to success in major sales.

- Demonstrating Capability:
	- Show how your product or service meets Explicit Needs only.
	- The client is the one telling YOU the Benefits of your solution.

- Obtaining Commitment
	- Check you've covered the buyer's key concerns.
	- Summarize the Benefits.
	- Propose an appropriate level of commitment.

- Focus on the Investigating Stage.
- Analyze your product in problem-solving terms.
- Plan, do, and review.
- It's all about the minute details.
