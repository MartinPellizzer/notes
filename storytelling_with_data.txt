	2. what do you want your audience to know or do?
	3. how can you use data to help make your point?

	- your audience
		- be specific
	- you
		- how your audience will perceive you as a person?
		- it's your first interraction? what's your relationship?
		- do they already trust you as an expert?
		- let your usdience be the hero of your story

- what
	- action
		- your goal is to get them to act accordingly to your goals
		- you are the expert. you know better than them what's best for them
		- be confident. your advice is backed up by data
		- ask for action after you made your point
		- check book for list of action words
	- mechanism
		- how you communicate?
		- how many details?
		- live presentation
			- high control > low details
			- don't just read slides
			- practice makes perfect
		- written doc/email
			- low control > high details
		- slideument
			- find the right balance betwessn live/writte and write a single document
	- tone
		- celebrative
		- light a fire
		- lighthearted
		- serious

- how
	- use data as supporting evidence of your case
	- don't hide nonsupporting data, it's very risky business
	- use non supporting data to add validity to your supporting data

- case study (book)

	- answer the "so what?" question in 1 paragrah and in 1 sentence

CHOOSING AN EFFECTIVE VISUAL
-----------------------
- best visuals
	- simple text
	- scatterplot
	- table
	- line
	- heatmap
	- slopegraph
	- vertical bar
	- horizontal bar
	- stacked vertical bar
	- stacked horizontal bar
	- waterfall
	- square area

- simple text
	- when you have just a number or two, use simple text, not graphs
	- make it prominent
	- few supporting words
	- these numbers get a lot of attention
	- are more immediate
	- don't use them for time relationships

- tables
	- great for comparing values
	- great for quick indexing
	- good to communicate multiple different units of measure
	- good to communicate to mixed audience whose members will each look for their particular row of interest
	- don't use in live presentations, you lose the audience attention
	- use easier to scan graphs in live presentations
	- don't use heavy borders or shading, don't let the table compete with the data for attention
	- only light or no borders
	- book stephen few, show me the numbers

- heatmap
	- in addition to numbers you use colors to convey the relative magnitude
	- use color saturation
	- help you find points of interest
	- higher saturation higher number
	- the lower the saturation, the lower the number

- graphs
	- while tables interact with our verbal system, graphs interact with our visual system
	- they are faster to process
	- well designed graphs are quicker to scan and get the point across than tables
	- 4 categories: points, lines, bars, area

- points
	- scatterplot
		- useful for showing the relationship between two things
		- more frequently used in scientific fields
		- they may seem intimidating if you are not used to it
		- less frequent in the business world
		- e.g. manage a bus fleet and understand the relationship between miles driven and cost per mile x bus
		- e.g. linear regression (to find average) and then classification to find the worst performers

- lines
	- to plot continuous data
	- relationship between point data (e.g. values over time)
	- line graph
		- can show single or multiple series of data
		- the time must be in constant intervalls
		- you can use it for summary statistic, like average
	- slopegraph 
		- use it when you have 2 time periods
		- just 1 line
		- not standard graphs in most applications

- bars
	- great for categorical data (groups)
	- they are very common and easy to understand
	- less learning curve for your audience
	- easy for the eyes to read
	- easy to see which category is bigger and smaller
	- always have a zero baseling, or misleading
	- the more you want to convey the bigger picture, the less the data (clutter) to convey
	- don't cheat by using magnified scale
	- the bars width should be wider than the whitespaces
	- don't make them too wide, make them just right
	- vertical bar chart
		- can be single, multiple series
		- more series, more difficult to focus
		- order series by relevancy and focus
	- stacked vertical bar chart
		- more limited use cases
		- allow you to compare totals across categories
		- see the subcomponent pieces within a given category
		- visualli overwhelming soon
		- can include absolute numbers when makes sense
	- waterfsll chart
		- to pull apartpieces of a stacked bar chart to focus on one at a time
		- uncommon to many softwares
	- horizontal bar chart
		- the go-to graph for categorical data
		- extremely easy to read
		- useful if categorical labels are long
		- we process informations from top to bottom, left to right
		- single series or multiple
		- order bars by logic (eg age group)
		- or by biggest or smallest category
	- stacked horizontal bar chart
		- give a sense of the subcomponent pieces
		- structured by absolute value or sum to 100%
		- 100% if you want to compare biggest and smallest components because they have a constant baseline

- area
	- avoid most area graphs
	- humans are not good with giving quantitative value to two-dimensional space
	- one exception, visualize numbers of vastly different magnitudes
	- you can create more compact visualizations than barcharts

- other types of graphs
	- choose the graph type that will enable you toclearly ger you message accross you audience
	- infographics, get inspired by data journalism (new york times and national geographic)

- to be avoided
	- pie charts
	- donut charts
	- 3d
	- secondary y-axes

- pie charts
	- are evil
	- human eyes are not good at describing quantitative values on a 2-dim space
	- hard for people to read
	- use horizontal bar charts insteadkJ:w

- donut chart
	- even worse than pie charts
	- even harder to compare

- 3d charts
	- add perspective, misleaded visualization
	- skew, tilting, and depth are very harmful
	- never use 3d
	- only if you are plotting a third dimention

- secondary yj-axis
	- genrally not a good idea

CLUTTER IS YOUR ENEMY
-----------------------
- every new element you add takes up cognitive load
- only put the essential to convey your message 
- be minimalist
- cognitive load during meetings is scarce
- remove ink on paper for stuff that info, or even detract info
- the more people perceive you graph as hard to read, the less they will read it
- minimize the perceived cognitive load
!!! less is more

# clutter
- it takes up space, but don't increase understanding
- makes your visual appear more complicated than necessary
- uncomfortable user experience
- people don't want to take the time to dig deep

# gestalt principles of visual perception
1. proximity
	- object close together appear they belong together
2. similarity
	- object of similar color, shape, size, orientate appear to be in the same group
3. enclosure
	- objects physically enclosed together apper to be in the same group
4. closure
	- when parts of a whole are mission, out eyes fill the gap (aka remove unnecessary borders)
5. continuity
	- similar to closure, the eyes separates shapes by paths
6. connection
	- objects physically connected appear to be in the same group (stroger than 2. similarities)

# lack of visual order
- good design fades into the background
1. alignment
	- tend to avoid center align, no reference line
	- the goal is to create clean lines
	- people usually read in 'z' pattern
	- on top-left tell users how to read the graph
2. white space
	- don't fear whitespaces
	- stop feeling the urge of filling every empty space on the canva
	- white spaces in visual communication = pauses in public speaking (important)
	- margin should remain free of thext and visuals
	- don't stretch the visuals to fill the entire space
	- use white spaces for emphasis
	- if something is really important, even make it the only element on the page

non-strategic use of contrast
	- clear contrast
	- focus their attention
	- lack of clear contrast = visual clutter
	- if you want an element to stand out, all the other elements must be similar
	- busy, confusing, exausting

decluttering: step-by-step
	1. remove chart borders
	2. remove gridlines
	3. remove data markers (e.g. the dots in a line chart)
	4. clean up axis labels
	5. label data directly (less legend)
	6. leverage consistent color

in closing
	- more info, more cognitive load

FOCUS YOUR AUDIENCE'S ATTENTION
-----------------------
- preattentive attributes
	- size
	- color
	- position

	1. help direct your audience's attention to where you want them to focus it
	2. can be used to create a visual hierarchy of elements to lead your audience

- you see with your brain

- a brief lesson on memory
	- iconic memory
		- super fast
		- survival mechanism
	- short-term memory
		- limited in size
		- graphs with 10 different colors are overwhelming
	- long-term memory
		- lifetime
		- verbal memory
		- visual memory

- preattentive attributes signal where to look
	- color (for example)
	- orientation
	- shape
	- length
	- width
	- size
	- curvature
	- markes
	- enclosure
	- hue
	- intensity
	- position
	- motion

	- people perceive long lines to be greater in value than short lines (barchart)

	1. drawing your audence attention quickly
	2. create visual hierarchy ofinformation

- PA in text
	- color
	- size
	- outline
	- bold
	- italics
	- separate spatially
	- underline
	
	- you have only 3-8 seconds to convince your audience to read
	- use PA to make your point quickly

- PA in graphs
	- warning: highlighting one aspect can make others harder to see
	
	- size
		- relative size = relative importance
		- important = make it big
	- color
		- when used sparingly > one of the most powerful attention tool
		- start with gray, then add 1 color to grab attention
		- when in doubt, use blue
			- most people like it
			- great for colorblindness
			- prints well on black-and-white
		1. use color sparingly
		2. use color consistently
		3. design with color blind in mind
		4. be thoughtful of the tone the color conveys
		5. consider whether to leverage brand colors
	- position on page
		- people read in z-flow
		- put the most important stuff on the lef-top corner

- in closing
	- use preattentive attributes sparingly
	- they are powerful stuff
